# Dust and Respirable Crystalline Silica Management

## Why it matters
Airborne dust from drilling, blasting, crushing, hauling, and processing contains **respirable
crystalline silica (RCS)**, which causes silicosis and other lung disease. Air quality is a
**principal hazard** managed under the operation's air quality / dust management plan, with
RCS exposures controlled below the WA workplace exposure standard.

## Exposure standard and monitoring
The operation monitors worker exposure with personal air sampling across **Similar Exposure
Groups (SEGs)** and area monitoring at fixed points. Results are compared to the regulated
8-hour exposure standard for RCS, trended over time, and reported to workers. Health
surveillance (including chest imaging and lung function) is provided for at-risk roles.

## Engineering and operational controls (higher order)
Following the hierarchy of controls, dust is controlled at the source before relying on PPE:
- **Water carts** and road watering to suppress haul-road dust; dust suppressants on high-traffic roads.
- **Wet drilling** or dust collection/extraction on drill rigs.
- Enclosed, **pressurised, filtered operator cabs** with maintained seals and filters.
- Sprays and enclosures at crushers, transfer points, and screens; covered conveyors.
- Wind monitoring; high-dust activities are curtailed in adverse wind conditions.

## Respiratory protection (last line)
Where exposure may still exceed the standard, a fit-tested respirator appropriate to the task
is mandatory (minimum **P2**; higher protection for high-exposure tasks). Users must be
clean-shaven at the respirator seal, and the program includes training, fit-testing, and
maintenance.

## Housekeeping
Do not dry-sweep settled dust — it re-suspends RCS. Use wet methods or vacuum equipment fitted
with appropriate (HEPA-class) filtration. Keep cab seals and filters maintained so the cab
stays a clean-air refuge.
