# Fatigue Risk Management

## Why fatigue is a principal risk
Fatigue impairs judgement, reaction time, and decision-making, and is a contributing factor
in many mobile-plant incidents. On a fly-in fly-out (FIFO) operation with 12-hour shifts and
rotating day/night rosters, fatigue is managed as a recognised hazard under the operation's
**Fatigue Risk Management Plan**, consistent with WA mining safety guidance.

## Shared responsibility
- **The operation** designs rosters, work hours, and break schedules to limit fatigue, and
  provides quality accommodation and quiet sleeping conditions at camp.
- **Workers** must present fit for work, use rest opportunities, and report fatigue.

## Roster and hours controls
- Standard shifts are 12 hours; continuous work beyond a shift requires supervisor approval
  and a fatigue assessment.
- A minimum break is provided between shifts to allow adequate sleep, travel, and meals.
- Night-shift workers are monitored more closely in the early-morning circadian low (roughly
  02:00–06:00), when alertness is lowest.

## Fitness for work
At the start of each shift, supervisors confirm each worker is fit for work. Anyone reporting
fatigue, illness, or the effects of medication or other substances must be **stood down** and
reassigned to a safe task or sent to rest. Fatigue is a reportable hazard and is recorded in
the shift handover log.

## Controls for operators
- Take scheduled breaks; do not skip them to "push through."
- Use the in-cab **fatigue detection technology** (operator-facing camera / eye-closure
  monitoring) fitted to haul trucks and dozers; respond to alerts by stopping safely.
- If you feel drowsy, park safely, notify your supervisor by radio, and take a break.

## Journey management
Driving to or from site is part of the fatigue picture. Long commutes after night shift are
high-risk; use the camp, scheduled transport, or a fatigue break rather than driving tired.
