# Shift Handover Procedure

## Why handover matters
Most operational surprises happen because information was lost between crews. A structured
handover ensures the incoming shift inherits an accurate picture of hazards, equipment status,
and work in progress. Handover is a controlled activity, not a casual chat.

## Timing
The outgoing supervisor prepares the handover during the last hour of the shift. The formal
handover meeting occurs at crew change and runs for no more than 15 minutes. Both supervisors
sign the handover log.

## Handover content
The handover must cover, as a minimum:

- **Safety**: open hazards, incidents and near-misses this shift, exclusion zones, active permits.
- **Production**: tonnes moved against plan, blocks in progress, grade-control notes.
- **Equipment**: machines down, defects raised, units on restricted operation or isolation.
- **People**: crew numbers, fatigue concerns, inductions or visitors on site.
- **Environment**: weather impacts, dust or water-management actions, spills.
- **Outstanding actions** and what the incoming shift is expected to prioritise.

## Equipment status board
The equipment status board is the single source of truth for machine availability. The
outgoing shift updates it before handover. Any machine tagged out must show the reason, the
work order number, and the expected return-to-service time. Isolations and lockouts are
verified against the isolation register.

## Weather and ground conditions
Handover explicitly covers weather that affects operations — heavy rain affecting haul-road
condition and pit-slope stability, lightning within the alert radius (which suspends crane
lifts and refuelling), and cyclone watch/warning status during the wet season.

## Fatigue and fitness for work
Supervisors confirm each incoming crew member is fit for work. Anyone reporting fatigue,
illness, or the effects of medication is stood down and reassigned to a safe task. Fatigue is
a reportable hazard captured in the handover log.
