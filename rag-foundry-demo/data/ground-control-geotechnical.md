# Ground Control and Geotechnical Management

## Principal hazard
Uncontrolled fall or failure of ground is a **principal hazard** capable of causing multiple
fatalities. It is managed under the operation's **Ground Control Management Plan (GCMP)**,
overseen by a geotechnical engineer, consistent with WA mining safety requirements.

## Pit slope design
Pit walls are designed with controlled **batter (face) angles, berm (catch bench) widths, and
overall slope angles** based on rock-mass and structural data. Berms catch rockfall and are
kept clear of spoil so they perform as designed. Final wall positions are surveyed against the
design and deviations are reviewed by the geotechnical engineer.

## Monitoring
Slope stability is monitored continuously using **slope stability radar**, prism surveys, and
piezometers for groundwater pressure. **Trigger Action Response Plans (TARPs)** define
green/amber/red thresholds: as movement accelerates past a threshold, defined actions follow —
increased monitoring, restricted access, or evacuation of personnel and equipment from below
the affected wall.

## Working near walls and faces
- Do not work or park under an unsupported or unscaled face.
- Observe the **stand-off distance** from crest and toe; barricade and sign exclusion areas.
- Report cracks, rilling, water seepage, or fresh rockfall immediately — these are warning signs.
- After heavy rain or a blast, faces are inspected and scaled before work resumes below them.

## Tip-head and dump management
Dumping at the tip head follows the dump management plan: a windrow (safety berm) of adequate
height is maintained, spotters direct trucks, and edge protection prevents over-edge events.
Ground is assessed for stability and subsidence before dumping continues.

## Excavations and trenching
Excavations are battered back or shored to prevent collapse, with safe access/egress and
exclusion of mobile plant from the edge. A permit and a competent person are required for
penetration and excavation work.
