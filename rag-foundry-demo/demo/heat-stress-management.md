# Heat Stress Management

## Why it matters
Work in the Pilbara regularly exceeds 40 °C in summer. Heat stress can cause heat exhaustion
and life-threatening heat stroke, and it compounds fatigue and impairs judgement. Working in
heat is managed under the operation's heat stress management plan.

## Acclimatisation
Workers new to site or returning after time away are **acclimatised** gradually over the first
days, with lighter duties and more frequent breaks, before undertaking strenuous work in the heat.

## Hydration
- Drink water regularly — about a cup (200–250 mL) every 15–20 minutes during heat, before you
  feel thirsty.
- Electrolyte replacement is provided for prolonged or strenuous work.
- Avoid excessive caffeine; do not rely on energy drinks.

## Work/rest cycles
When the measured heat stress index reaches defined thresholds, **work/rest cycles** are applied:
more frequent rest in shade or air-conditioning, rotation of strenuous tasks, and rescheduling
heavy work to the cooler parts of the day. At extreme thresholds, non-essential outdoor work is
suspended.

## Recognising heat illness
Signs include heavy sweating, headache, dizziness, nausea, cramps, and confusion. Heat stroke
(hot dry skin, collapse, confusion) is a medical emergency.

## If someone shows symptoms
1. Stop work and move the person to a cool, shaded, or air-conditioned area.
2. Give water if they are conscious and able to drink.
3. Cool them with water, fanning, and loosened clothing.
4. Call the site medic immediately on the emergency channel; treat suspected heat stroke as a
   medical emergency.

## Controls and PPE
Use shade structures and air-conditioned cabs and crib huts, schedule work to avoid peak heat,
and wear light, breathable high-visibility clothing. Report anyone struggling in the heat to
your supervisor.
